How the system works — data pipeline & detection rules

The platform runs as an automated cycle: it imports the latest game data on a schedule, runs the fraud rules against it, builds an investigation case for any player that matches, and saves that case to a separate database where it is timestamped and tracked. This document explains that pipeline, then breaks down each of the five rules with examples.

The end-to-end pipeline
1
Scheduled data export (API → database)

On a set cycle, the scheduler calls the provider's reporting API, downloads the latest reports, and imports them into the data warehouse — the source tables that hold accounts, cash sessions, and tournament results.

Example: every night the scheduler pulls "Account Information", "Cash Table Summary" and "SNG / Twister / MTT" reports and loads the new rows into the warehouse.
2
Scheduled rule cycle (the scan)

Each scan cycle, the engine loads the active rules and their thresholds, then runs Rules 1–5 in sequence against the freshly imported data. Only rules switched on in the settings screen are evaluated.

Example: the cycle starts, confirms Rules 1–5 are enabled, and begins testing every player against each rule's criteria.
3
Build the case (pull supporting data)

When a rule matches a player, the engine pulls that player's supporting detail from the warehouse — profile, financials (profit, ROI, rake), playing partners and recent sessions — and assembles a full case with a risk score, a category, a tag, and a plain-English reason explaining why it fired.

Example: Rule 1 flags a player; the engine then attaches their lifetime profit, rake, win rates and top partners so an analyst sees the whole picture in one place.
4
Save & timestamp (separate case database)

The finished case is written to a separate case-management database — distinct from the raw game-data warehouse. There it is timestamped (created and last-updated) and tracked through its investigation status. If a later cycle re-flags the same player, the engine updates the existing open case instead of creating a duplicate.

Example: the case is stored as "#1043 — Open, created 14 Jun 02:10", giving a permanent, time-stamped audit trail separate from the source data.
Why two databases? The warehouse holds raw, re-importable game data and is refreshed every cycle. The case database is the system of record for investigations — it preserves each case and its timestamps even as the underlying data is refreshed, so findings are never overwritten.
The five detection rules

Each rule targets a different collusion or fraud signature. Defaults shown below are configurable in the settings screen.

Rule 1 · Chip Dumping
Cash margin — new account

The area: In fair cash poker a player's profit is only a tiny slice of the money they wager (about −2% to +3%). Winning a large percentage of total bets signals a partner deliberately losing chips to them — chip dumping.

How it flags: margin = total profit ÷ total bets × 100. Flag when margin ≥ 50% and total bets ≥ 100.

Player Profit Bets Margin Outcome
A 8,000 10,000 80% Flagged
B 90 60 150% Clear — bets below 100
C 200 10,000 2% Clear — normal margin
Case reason produced: “Rule 1 [Cash]: Cash margin 80.00% (floor 50.0%) — Σ profit ÷ Σ bets; Σ bets 10,000 (min 100).”
Rule 2 · New Account High Win
Major income — “% Win” spike

The area: A days-old account that instantly books a huge return is a hallmark of a receiver account — value funnelled into a fresh identity, or bonus/jackpot abuse.

How it flags: account signed up ≤ 2 days ago and a session shows > 500% win and the win is ≥ 50 in value.

Player Account age % Win Win value Outcome
A 1 day 1,200% 300 Flagged
B 10 days 1,200% 300 Clear — account too old
C 1 day 1,200% 20 Clear — win value too small
Case reason produced: “Rule 2 [Major]: ‘% Win’ 1200.00% (floor 500.0%), Win 300.00 (min 50.0), Buy 25.00; signup ≤ 2d.”
Rules 3–5 · Common Games (shared tournaments)
These three rules use the same idea across three tournament formats: two players who keep entering the same tournaments far more than chance allows are likely a collusion team sharing a table. A pair must share ≥ 5 tournaments and reach 30% overlap (shared ÷ that player's total in the format). The only difference is whether both or either player must hit 30%.
Rule 3 · Common Games
Twister overlap — both players must be concentrated

The area: Twisters are tiny (a handful of players per game), so coincidental overlap is common. To avoid false alarms, Rule 3 only fires when both players are heavily concentrated on each other — a strong sign they are deliberately joining the same games.

Scenario Shared A overlap B overlap Outcome
A=12, B=8 6 50% 75% Flagged — both ≥ 30%
A=12, B=100 6 50% 6% Clear — B below 30%
Reads as: Player A shared 6 of their 12 Twisters with Player B, who shared the same 6 of just 8 — both are overwhelmingly playing each other.
Rule 4 · Common Games
MTT overlap — either player concentrated

The area: Multi-table tournaments have large fields, so a high-volume grinder can have a low overlap % even while a colluder shadows them. Rule 4 therefore fires if either player crosses 30% — catching the low-volume account that almost only plays the target's games.

Scenario Shared A overlap B overlap Outcome
A=40, B=5 5 12.5% 100% Flagged — B ≥ 30%
A=40, B=60 5 12.5% 8.3% Clear — neither ≥ 30%
Reads as: Player A is a high-volume grinder (40 MTTs); Player B has played 5 MTTs — all 5 alongside A. A alone looks innocent, but B exists almost entirely to play A's games.
Rule 5 · Common Games
SNG overlap — either player concentrated

The area: Sit & Go tournaments fill on demand, making it easy for two accounts to repeatedly land in the same game. As with MTTs, Rule 5 fires if either player's overlap reaches 30%.

Scenario Shared A overlap B overlap Outcome
A=30, B=10 8 26.7% 80% Flagged — B ≥ 30%
A=30, B=10 3 10% 30% Clear — shared below 5
Reads as: 8 shared SNGs clears the “at least 5” floor, and Player B played 8 of their 10 SNGs with Player A (80%) — flagged. In the second row only 3 are shared, so it never qualifies regardless of overlap %.
How a case is scored
Each rule a player breaks adds points to their risk score (35 points each by default). A case is promoted for analyst review once the score reaches the minimum trigger (100) — so a player usually needs more than one signal to surface. Every threshold is adjustable in the rule-settings screen.
